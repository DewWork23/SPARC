The discussion, led primarily by Valerie, traced the consortium's significant growth from a small, grant-funded group of four entities in 2019 to a large coalition of over 70 agencies.

Consortium History and Growth

Origins (2019)

The consortium began with a HRSA planning grant written with support from UNCP. The initial focus was on implementing grant deliverables with just 4 core partners.

Original Partners

  • UNCP University of North Carolina Pembroke
  • Robinson Health Healthcare Partner
  • DFS Department of Family Services
  • 4th Partner Initial Core Member

Evolution Over Time

The consortium's focus shifted from strict grant deliverables to addressing broader community needs. While other coalitions focused on issues like diabetes, this group took the lead on substance use initiatives.

Key Phases:

  • 2019: Planning grant phase with initial 4 partners
  • Implementation Phase: Expanded to implementation grant with growing membership
  • Growth Phase: Membership expanded to 70+ agencies
  • Sustainability Phase: Current focus on long-term vision beyond grant requirements

Vision Evolution

The consortium is transitioning from "next 3 years" planning to long-term sustainability thinking, moving beyond checking off grant deliverables to creating a member-driven vision independent of grant requirements.

Key Successes, Accomplishments, and Strengths

Infrastructure & Services

  • Ron Barnes Center: 77-bed facility opened in July (currently at 60 beds, targeting full capacity by year-end)
  • County Relations: Significantly improved relationship with county government
  • Access to Care: Enhanced access to residential housing and treatment options
  • Transportation: Exploration of Uber Health to address transportation barriers

Membership and Engagement

Diverse Membership Includes:

  • Trillium Health Resources
  • Hospital representatives
  • Public health officials
  • Sororities and community-based organizations
  • Family members and people with lived experience

High Engagement: Consistent attendance and active participation at meetings, successfully breaking down silos between organizations and reducing competition among service providers.

Programs and Impact

  • Training Programs: "March's Madness" series delivers 4-6 high-quality training events annually
  • Overdose Reduction: Measurable impact on reducing overdoses in the community
  • Stigma Reduction: Progress in addressing stigma through recovery-oriented language initiatives
  • Information Hub: Comprehensive resource and data sharing among members
  • Grant Support: Assistance provided for member organizations in grant writing
  • Events: Movie Night and other community engagement initiatives

Collaborative Strengths

  • Strong collaboration and standardized data systems
  • Court system implementing SAFER approach
  • Trillium's integrated approach to treatment and housing
  • Active advocacy and awareness initiatives

Current Barriers and Challenges

Stakeholder Engagement

Difficulty Getting Key Stakeholders to the Table:

  • Law Enforcement: Need consistent participation
  • Elected Officials: Need stronger representation
  • Faith-Based Organizations: Underrepresented at meetings
  • Health Department: Engagement remains limited

Overall Trend: Some signs of declining engagement that need to be addressed.

Funding and Resources

  • General Funding Constraints: Limited resources for expanding initiatives
  • County Competition: Competition between county and consortium for available funds
  • Proposal Rejection: County's rejection of combined proposal creating fragmentation

Service Gaps

Critical Service Needs:

  • Mental Health Treatment: Lack of quality mental health treatment, especially for co-occurring disorders
  • Medicaid Limitations: Restrictions affecting service delivery and coverage
  • Transportation: Remains a persistent community challenge despite progress with Uber Health exploration

Missing Stakeholders

Primary Gaps:

  • Hospitals (need better representation)
  • More individuals with lived experience in core team roles

Communication Gaps:

  • Community members not fully aware of available resources
  • Need better information flow between leadership team and member organizations

Future Vision and Strategic Direction

Organizational Development

501(c)(3) Status: Pursue nonprofit status through NC Center for Nonprofits to enhance sustainability and funding opportunities

Structural Improvements:

  • Shared Staffing Models: Explore collaborative staffing for sustainability
  • Volunteer to Paid Transition: Move from volunteer-driven to more sustained staffing model
  • Enhanced Coordinator Support: Increase support for existing coordinator roles
  • Meeting Optimization: Better coordinate voice and action meetings, consider combining when appropriate

Required Strategic Shifts

  • Goals beyond immediate grant deliverables (SDOH)
  • Enhanced community counselor role with increased support
  • Focus on treatment and recovery initiatives beyond compliance

Program Strategy

Key Initiatives:

  • Ongoing Stigma Campaign: Launch sustained effort to reduce stigma around substance use
  • Multiple Pathways to Recovery: Promote diverse approaches beyond traditional treatment
  • Participation Incentives: Create incentive programs to boost member engagement
  • Information Hub: Strengthen role as central resource for SDOH and community needs
  • Back to In-Person: Focus on rebuilding in-person connections and engagement

Vision for Growth

"Regionalization of Robeson"

Improve perception of Robeson County across the region while strengthening local foundation first. The approach: "Work on our own house before we expand."

Growth Priorities:

  • Continue increasing membership with diverse representation
  • Enhance participation strategies moving members from passive to active roles
  • Develop member-driven vision independent of grant requirements
  • Position consortium as SDOH/social determinant solution hub for community

Collaborative Grant Proposals

Develop proposals around initiatives showing prevention funding utilization, including prevention, treatment, and recovery components that benefit the broader consortium membership.

Member Engagement and Leadership

Leadership Team Assessment

What's Working Well:

  • Unity & Shared Vision: Strong alignment on goals and direction
  • Mutual Respect: Collaborative and respectful working relationships
  • Expertise: Strong leaders including Valerie Bradshaw, Carolyn & Robinson, Trillium
  • Diversity: Diverse representation across sectors and communities

Areas Needing Improvement:

  • Information Flow: Need clearer decision-making process between leadership team and organizations
  • Role Coordination: Better coordination around roles and responsibilities
  • Lived Experience: More representation from people with lived experience in core team roles

Engagement Enhancement Strategies

Full Consortium Voting: Implement voting on new initiatives to give all members a voice in decision-making

Proposed Engagement Tactics:

  • Rotating Facilitation: Share facilitation responsibilities among member agencies
  • Member-Driven Agendas: Involve members in agenda setting and planning
  • Voice and Action Coordination: Better integrate people with lived experience into meetings
  • Active Participation: Move members from passive attendees to active contributors
  • Agenda Involvement: Ask "Who from [organization] can lead this agenda item?"

Missing Stakeholder Outreach

Valerie's Action: Reach out to local hospital contact to encourage participation

Target Stakeholder Groups:

  • Hospitals (especially local contacts with familiarity with consortium work)
  • RISE organization
  • Community colleges (including Robeson Community College)
  • Faith-based organizations
  • Elected officials
  • Public health representatives

Positioning Lived Experience

Critical shift needed: Move people with lived experience from bottom of agenda to more prominent positioning in both meetings and leadership roles. Their perspective is essential for authentic recovery-oriented approaches.
