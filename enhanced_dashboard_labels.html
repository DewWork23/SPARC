Understanding the Regional Crisis

SPARC

Southeastern Prevention & Addiction Recovery Resource Center

SPARC aims to unify and strengthen community efforts to combat substance use and support recovery in Robeson County. By fostering robust partnerships and collaborative initiatives, SPARC addresses both immediate and long-term needs, enhancing prevention, treatment and recovery services for individuals and families affected by substance use disorders.

Why This Region Needs Focused Attention

üî¥ Elevated Death Rates

Richmond County's rate of 89.8 per 100k is more than 2x the state average (41.0), representing one of the highest overdose death rates in North Carolina.

üìà Significant Regional Burden

The six-county Focus Region accounts for approximately 7-8% of all NC overdose deaths, despite representing a smaller portion of the state's population.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Polysubstance Use Is More Dangerous:
  • Unpredictable Interactions: Combining substances exponentially increases overdose risk and makes naloxone less effective
  • Treatment Complexity: Multiple dependencies require comprehensive, longer-term treatment - not just opioid-focused MAT
  • Stimulant-Opioid "Speedballs": Cocaine/meth + fentanyl create unique overdose patterns that may not respond to standard naloxone dosing
  • Depressant Stacking: Alcohol, benzos, or gabapentin with fentanyl suppress respiratory function further, making overdoses more likely to be fatal
üí° Implications for Harm Reduction & Treatment:
  • Fentanyl test strips alone insufficient - need comprehensive drug checking technology
  • Higher naloxone doses required for polysubstance overdoses - distribute multi-dose kits
  • Integrated treatment must address cocaine, meth, alcohol, and prescription drug use - not just opioids
  • Education campaigns should warn about specific dangerous combinations (cocaine+fentanyl, alcohol+fentanyl)
  • Never use alone messaging critical - polysubstance overdoses develop faster and are harder to reverse

Success Stories & Effective Interventions

üìâ -53%

Richmond County ED Visit Decline (YTD 2025)

From 57 visits (YTD 2024) ‚Üí 27 visits (YTD 2025)

Progress is possible. While the regional crisis continues, certain counties and interventions have shown measurable success. These examples provide evidence-based blueprints for scaling effective approaches.

üéØ Richmond County: A Case Study in Reduction

Richmond County reduced overdose ED visits by 53% year-over-year (57 visits YTD 2024 ‚Üí 27 visits YTD 2025). While specific intervention details require further investigation, this decline likely reflects a combination of:

  • ‚úÖ MAT expansion - Increased access to buprenorphine/methadone in community health centers
  • ‚úÖ Naloxone distribution - Standing orders at pharmacies, community distribution programs
  • ‚úÖ EMS/First responder training - Enhanced overdose response protocols
  • ‚úÖ Harm reduction outreach - Mobile units, peer support, fentanyl test strips
  • ‚úÖ Coordinated care - ED-initiated buprenorphine, warm handoffs to treatment

Recommendation: Conduct Richmond County site visit to document successful interventions and create replication toolkit for other counties.

üíä MAT Expansion Works

Counties with increased MAT capacity (especially same-day buprenorphine access) show reduced overdose deaths. Evidence from other states: 10-20% reduction in deaths within 1-2 years of MAT expansion.

üöë ED-Initiated Buprenorphine

Starting buprenorphine in the ED and providing warm handoffs to outpatient treatment increases treatment retention by 2-3x compared to referral alone (JAMA evidence).

üíâ Harm Reduction Programs

Syringe services programs (SSP) reduce HIV/HCV transmission by 50%+ and increase treatment entry by connecting people to services. Counties with SSPs show measurable public health benefits.

üì± Telehealth MAT

Virtual buprenorphine prescribing removes geographic barriers in rural counties. Evidence: Similar treatment outcomes to in-person care, with greater reach and lower no-show rates.

üí° What We Can Learn:
  • Treatment access is key: Counties that reduce barriers to MAT (same-day access, telehealth, Medicaid acceptance) see measurable reductions in harm
  • Naloxone saves lives: Widespread distribution through pharmacies, community programs, and first responders prevents fatal overdoses
  • Harm reduction works: Meeting people where they are (SSPs, mobile units, peer support) reduces deaths and increases treatment engagement
  • Coordination multiplies impact: When EMS, EDs, treatment providers, and harm reduction programs work together, outcomes improve dramatically
üöÄ Scaling What Works: Regional Action Plan
  1. Document Richmond County success - Interview stakeholders, identify specific interventions, create replication guide
  2. Expand ED-initiated buprenorphine - Train ED providers at Cumberland, Robeson, Scotland, and Columbus county hospitals
  3. Universal naloxone access - Standing orders at all pharmacies, community distribution programs in high-risk areas
  4. Mobile MAT/telehealth - Deploy mobile units or telehealth hubs in rural counties with limited treatment capacity
  5. Harm reduction expansion - Establish SSPs in Robeson and Cumberland, expand peer support networks
  6. Use opioid settlement funds strategically - Prioritize evidence-based interventions with demonstrated ROI (MAT expansion, naloxone distribution, harm reduction)

Call to Action: Strategic Recommendations

Based on the data analysis and regional trends, we have identified priority interventions, resource allocation recommendations, and collaboration opportunities to address the overdose crisis in our Focus Region.

Priority Interventions Needed

üéØ Immediate Priorities (Next 3-6 Months)

  • Richmond County Intensive Response: Deploy additional harm reduction resources, expand naloxone distribution, and increase treatment capacity in Richmond County given its 89.8 rate
  • ED Follow-Up Programs: Implement or enhance peer recovery specialist programs in emergency departments across all counties to connect overdose survivors with treatment
  • Fentanyl Testing Strips: Mass distribution of fentanyl test strips and overdose prevention education, particularly targeting the 18-44 age group
  • Rural Access Initiatives: Address transportation barriers and telehealth expansion for rural counties (Richmond, Scotland, Bladen) where geographic isolation compounds the crisis

üìä Medium-Term Strategies (6-12 Months)

  • Data-Driven Resource Allocation: Use ED visit trends as early warning indicators to pre-emptively deploy resources before deaths increase
  • Treatment Capacity Expansion: Increase medication-assisted treatment (MAT) availability, particularly in counties showing persistent high rates
  • Community-Based Prevention: Expand evidence-based prevention programs targeting youth and young adults before substance use disorder develops
  • Recovery Support Services: Build peer support networks and recovery housing options to reduce relapse and support long-term recovery
  • Cross-Sector Collaboration: Strengthen partnerships between healthcare, law enforcement, social services, and community organizations

Resource Allocation Recommendations

High Priority Counties

Richmond & Scotland: Elevated rates with increasing trends require intensive intervention and additional resources

Moderate Priority Counties

Bladen & Columbus: Stabilized but elevated rates; maintain current interventions while monitoring trends

Sustaining Success Counties

Cumberland & Robeson: Recent improvements suggest interventions are working; maintain and document successful strategies for replication

Opioid Settlement Fund Priorities

  • Treatment Infrastructure: 30-40% of funds toward expanding MAT access and residential treatment capacity
  • Harm Reduction: 20-25% for naloxone distribution, syringe services, and fentanyl test strips
  • Prevention & Education: 15-20% for evidence-based prevention programs and community education
  • Recovery Support: 15-20% for peer support services, recovery housing, and wraparound services
  • Data & Evaluation: 10% for ongoing data collection, analysis, and program evaluation

  • Share Transparently: Regular public reporting builds accountability and community engagement
  • Use Data for Advocacy: Evidence-based arguments are essential for securing funding and policy support

We have the data. We have the evidence. We have the community commitment. Now we need coordinated action and sustained resources to save lives.
